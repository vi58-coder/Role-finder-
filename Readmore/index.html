Design Process, Prejudice and
First Contacts

This design thing is funny. I started out with a design for grids and clean workspaces. I thought it was faux intellectualism, some designers I knew then used to create some sort of "classism" because they used guides and grids, and I hated it because I knew it was all fake. They would look down on designers using CorelDraw and say things like "You won't get far" because they were using Illustrator. I always enjoyed the befuddlement when we would present deliverables and the Corel guys would have the best designs, or when I'd eyeball stuff and it'd be almost inch perfect.

Today, I use grids and guides a lot, have fallen in love with auto-layout, and realized its usefulness in my workflow. My layout and workspace(s) are less messy - I create a page for sandboxing and trying out stuff before I move them to the main design area.

Occasionally, I still design buttons with rectangles and text, and then do another sweep and convert the assets to components.

In the end, output is important, but processes help streamline and improve output. Learning the tool and tailoring it to your workflow gives you the best results.

They would look down on designers using CorelDraw and say things like "You won't get far" because they were using Illustrator.

This is just one random instance of the dangers of one-sided stories and how we interact with concepts and information based on our first experience(s) with them. The tool is important, but skill and principles supersede all else.
